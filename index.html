Patterns of Patrol

The world recently has been filled with news of racial discrimination within policing practices. Videos of traffic stops turning foul or even violent are unfortunately commonplace. Looking at traffic stop data, specifically for California and its cities, do any patterns based on race actually arise? Using data from the Stanford Open Policing Project, we can take a look at how traffic stops give insight into the biases of police.

Arrest Rates in California

Take a closer look at some of the cities in California. This time, look at the stop and arrest data.
Note that this is somewhat limited by the number of cities and stops that had the proper arrest data recorded

We can see that across every city, the arrest rate for traffic stops where the driver was black is the highest compared to every other race. Out of all of them Oakland seems to have the highest rate out of all of them. A whopping 14.5% of black drivers that were stopped ended up being arrested! Compare this to the arrest rate for white drivers: 7.7%. The arrest rate for black drivers is nearly double the rate for white drivers.

At an initial look this does seem to imply that -at least in Oakland- black drivers truly are unfairly treated by police at traffic stops. But in many real life cases, arguments are made that the police more often than not have just reason to make an arrest, or perhaps to even make a search in the first place, and that they are simply doing their job. Let's look at how often these reasons actually pop up.

Contraband Rates in Oakland

= Contraband Found

For black drivers that were stopped by police, around 14.5 percent, or around 15 out of 100, ended up having contraband found in the vehicle. That seems to match up closely with what we found for arrest rates, but let's take a look at the same statistic for white drivers. A little over 2% more at 17.1% of white drivers that were stopped were found with contraband. The percentages seem comparable, but it's important to remember black drivers were arrested at nearly double the rate!

Key Takeaways

It definitely does appear that -at the very least- certain cities experience discrimination due to race by the police. The arrest rates don't match the rates that contraband would be found. While you may see and hear many statistics about highly debated topics such as this, it's important to remember that all of these statistics don't exist in a vacuum. Even for just one city, just looking at the rates at which contraband was found among black and white drivers wouldn't have been the full story. It's likely even more important to remember that these aren't just statistics, but actual lives being affected. We encourage you to explore the full data from the Stanford Open Policing Project and come up with your own findings!
